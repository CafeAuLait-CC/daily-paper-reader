1 Introduction 

Recent advances in diffusion and flow models have enabled high-fidelity and flexible image synthesis across diverse domains. Cor-respondingly, the precise alignment of these generic pre-trained models with human preferences or task-specific constraints remains a non-trivial challenge. To this end, extensive research has explored post-training preference alignment techniques, which leverage feed-back on model outputs to steer sampling trajectories toward desired endpoints. Such feedback can be derived from either direct human judgment [Liang et al . 2024; Xu et al . 2023] or automated ratings from reward models [Lin et al . 2024; Sun et al . 2025], both reflecting specific preferences regarding the generated content. To leverage outcome feedback for optimization, a straightforward approach is pairwise comparison, which increases the likelihood ratio of pre-ferred samples over non-preferred ones, termed Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [Huang et al . 2025; Majumder et al . 2024; Wal-lace et al . 2024]. However, such comparisons only provide ranking in-formation, making it difficult to distinguish between marginally and substantially better samples, thereby limiting optimization granular-ity. To address this, Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) [He et al . 2025; Li et al . 2025a; Liu et al . 2025] samples a group of outputs from the same prompt and learns relative advantages through intra-group normalization. This eliminates shared baseline interference while reducing variance, thus improving both training stability and performance ceiling. Specifically for flow models, existing approaches [Li et al . 2025b; Xue et al . 2025; Zhou et al . 2025b] typically convert the determinis-tic Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) sampling into Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE) sampling to introduce the exploratory randomness required by GRPO. Nevertheless, due to the inherently multi-step iterative denoising process of flow models and GRPO’s reliance on large group sizes for accurate intra-group advantage estimation, generating a rollout group remains computationally ex-pensive. This compels existing methods to adopt significantly fewer training steps compared to inference (for instance, 16 training steps versus the default 50 inference steps for FLUX.1 dev). Consequently, each trajectory provides only a handful of supervised transitions, leaving the vast majority of intermediate denoising stages without direct reward guidance. Such temporal sparsity severely constrains fine-grained preference alignment, as the optimization signal is com-pressed into sparse velocity updates at isolated timesteps, rather than propagating across the fine-grained denoising trajectory. In this work, we introduce Pave-GRPO to address this limi-tation. Rather than generating expensive high-step samples, we maintain efficient few-step group sampling but decompose each coarse transition into equivalently consecutive sub-fragments, back-propagating optimization signals to a denser set of temporal stages. This design stems from two observations. First, the iterative de-noising process of a flow model is inherently a Markov process, in which each intermediate state serves as a sufficient statistic for the subsequent state, making these intermediate states reusable as shared targets for trajectory decomposition. Second, both pure SDE and hybrid ODE–SDE sampling yield tractable Gaussian prob-ability paths. This allows the likelihood of few-step samples to be evaluated exactly under varying step configurations, enabling the recycling of advantages from cheap rollouts as supervision signals for multi-step optimization. The resulting decoupling yields two gains: (i) expanded effective optimization horizon without increased sampling cost, lifting the preference alignment ceiling; and (ii) com-prehensive temporal supervision via shared intermediate regression targets, which distributes reward feedback across a denser set of temporal stages and enables finer-grained, more thorough prefer-ence optimization. Experiments across multiple settings confirm that Pave-GRPO consistently outperforms its competitors. 

2 Related work 2.1 Diffusion and flow models in visual generation 

Diffusion models [Bu et al . 2025; Dhariwal and Nichol 2021; Ho et al . 2020; Song et al . 2020a,b] have become a dominant paradigm in generative modeling by learning to reverse an iterative nois-ing process, enabling high-fidelity synthesis across diverse modali-ties including images [Labs 2024; Podell et al . 2023; Rombach et al .2022] and video [Chen et al . 2024; Guo et al . 2023; Wan et al . 2025; Zhou et al . 2025a]. To mitigate the computational burden of oper-ating in high-dimensional pixel spaces, Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) [Podell et al . 2023; Rombach et al . 2022] perform the de-noising process within a compressed latent representation. More recently, flow matching [Esser et al . 2024; Lipman et al . 2022; Liu et al. 2022] emerges as a compelling alternative framework, which learns a continuous velocity field that transports probability mass from a noise distribution to the data distribution. This paradigm has proven highly effective, achieving state-of-the-art performance in high-quality image generation, as evidenced by models such as Stable Diffusion 3 [Esser et al . 2024], FLUX series [Labs 2024, 2025], and Qwen-Image [Wu et al . 2025], as well as in large-scale video synthesis with models including HunyuanVideo [Team 2025], WAN series [Wan et al. 2025] and LongCat-Video [Team et al. 2025]. 

2.2 Preference alignment in flow models 

Aligning diffusion and flow models with human preferences has evolved from early PPO-based policy gradients [Black et al . 2023; Schulman et al . 2017; Xu et al . 2023] and DPO variants [Peng et al .2025; Rafailov et al . 2023; Wallace et al . 2024] toward more efficient online reinforcement learning frameworks, such as Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) [Shao et al . 2024]. To adapt GRPO for flow matching, foundational works like Flow-GRPO [Liu et al . 2025] and DanceGRPO [Xue et al . 2025] reformulate deterministic ODE sampling into equivalent SDE trajectories, facilitating stochastic exploration while preserving marginal distributions. Subsequent

For instance, Flow-CPS [Wang and Yu 2025] addresses the noise in-consistency in SDE sampling to prevent residual artifacts, while MixGRPO [Li et al . 2025a] introduces a hybrid ODE-SDE strategy to enhance training efficiency. Further improvements include tree-structured rollouts in BranchGRPO [Li et al . 2025b] for gradient con-centration, fine-grained credit assignment in TempFlow-GRPO [He et al . 2025] and Granular-GRPO [Zhou et al . 2025b], as well as dense multi-view reward mapping in MV-GRPO [Bu et al . 2026] with-out costly sample regeneration. This temporal sparsity intrinsically limits the granularity of preference alignment. To address this, our Pave-GRPO equivalently decomposes a low-step denoising trajectory into consecutive sub-fragments, decoupling the generation step budget from optimization granularity.

Backbone and baselines . All experiments are conducted on Flux.1-dev [Labs 2024], a widely adopted text-to-image backbone compris-ing 12B parameters. We compare against both conventional and recent methods, including Flow-GRPO [Liu et al . 2025], Dance-GRPO [Xue et al. 2025], and Flow-CPS [Wang and Yu 2025]. 

Evaluation metrics . For a comprehensive assessment of generation quality, we evaluate across three types of metrics: (i) CLIP [Radford et al. 2021] for image-text alignment; (ii) dedicated reward models: ImageReward [Xu et al . 2023], HPS-v2/v3 [Wu et al . 2023] [Ma et al . 2025], UnifiedReward-v1/v2 [Wang et al . 2025b], and Pick-Score [Kirstain et al . 2023]; and (iii) fine-grained semantic evaluation on UniGenBench [Wang et al . 2025a]. It leverages an MLLM-based assessor to score 10 primary dimensions (including attribute binding, spatial layout, world knowledge, text rendering, etc.), providing a holistic evaluation on conceptual fidelity, visual appeal, and overall text-image alignment. 

Training settings . We perform full-parameter training with a batch size of 8, using the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 2×10 −6.To enhance computational efficiency, we employ bfloat16 mixed precision for training acceleration. Our Pave-GRPO builds upon the variant of FlowGRPO [He et al . 2025], which adopts step-wise reward assessment to ensure reliable credit assignment and faster convergence, using a fixed group size of 12 for sample generation. All baselines adopt the same acceleration strategy for fair compari-son. These results validate that Pave-GRPO effectively har-nesses the reward signal to improve both subject fidelity and scene complexity without sacrificing overall visual harmony.

5.4 Analysis and Ablations 

Effect of multi-path optimization. Given that Pave-GRPO is built upon Flow-GRPO that also employs marginal-preserving SDE (F mar sde ), the comparison between Flow-GRPO and Pave-GRPO ( F mar sde )in Table 1 and Table 2 constitutes a direct ablation, in which the consistent superiority of Pave-GRPO across different dimensions val-idates its efficacy in advancing text-to-image preference alignment. The corresponding reward curves during training are also provided in Figure 5, corroborating its stable performance improvement. 

Sensitivity to decomposition factors. Since our method intro-duces a decomposition-factor set S that determines the sub-step granularity in the hybrid paths, we further assess its robustness by systematically varying S. Results in Table. 3 shows that the proposed methods consistently outperform the baseline under dif-ferent S, confirming the effectiveness of multi-path decomposition across various sub-step configurations. However, it is also observed that increasing |S| proportionally expands the number of hybrid paths, thereby raising computational overhead per-training step. Meanwhile, performance improvements exhibit diminishing returns and do not scale monotonically with larger S, indicating that ex-cessively fine-grained decomposition yields only marginal gains at the cost of increased training cost. Therefore, unless otherwise specified, we adopt S = {2, 3} for all comparative experiments in this paper, achieving a favorable balance between performance and computational efficiency. 

6 Conclusion 

In this work, we present Pave-GRPO, a novel framework that en-hances GRPO post-training for preference alignment of flow models. Instead of expensive high-step rollouts, it keeps lightweight, few-step pure-SDE sampling and breaks each coarse trajectory into consecutive smaller segments. Based on the Markov property of iterative denoising and the tractable Gaussian probability paths shared by ODE and SDE forms, it reuses cheap rollout advantages as dense supervision signals for multi-step optimization. This sepa-rates the generation step budget from the optimization granularity, extending the effective optimization range while implicitly enforc-ing piecewise velocity consistency through shared intermediate targets. As a result, the model achieves more comprehensive pref-erence alignment and stronger cross-metric generalization with no extra sampling cost. Experiments on FLUX.1-dev confirm that
