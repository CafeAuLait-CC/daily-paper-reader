Abstract. Video-driven human reaction generation aims to synthesize 3D human motions that directly react to observed video sequences, which is crucial for building human-like interactive AI systems. However, existing methods often fail to effectively leverage video inputs to steer human reaction synthesis, resulting in reaction motions that are mismatched with the content of video sequences. We reveal that this limitation arises from a severe relational distortion between visual observations and reaction types. In light of this, we propose MuSteerNet, a simple yet effective framework that generates 3D human reactions from videos via observation-reaction mutual steering. Specifically, we first propose a Prototype Feedback Steering mechanism to mitigate relational distortion by refining visual observations with a gated delta-rectification modulator and a relational margin constraint, guided by prototypical vectors learned from human reactions. We then introduce Dual-Coupled Reaction Refinement that fully leverages rectified visual cues to further steer the refinement of generated reaction motions, thereby effectively improving reaction quality and enabling MuSteerNet to achieve competitive performance. Extensive experiments and ablation studies validate the effectiveness of our method. Code coming soon: https://github.com/zhouyuan888888/MuSteerNet .

# 1 Introduction 

Human reaction generation, as a key problem in interactive modeling and gen-eration [4, 40, 60, 72, 75], aims to synthesize realistic 3D human motions that appropriately respond to stimuli observed from the environment, and is cru-cial for human–robot interaction, virtual and augmented reality, and embodied AI. [22, 37, 49, 55, 56, 60] has significantly improved reaction motion modeling for human-human interactions. Yet, generating human reactions that directly respond to videos remains challenging and largely underexplored, despite videos serving as a key visual modality through which machines perceive and understand the real world [29 –31, 38, 50, 77]. To bridge this gap, Yu et al. [63] introduced video-driven human reaction generation, a task that aims to enable models to perceive interaction intents from video sequences and synthesize plausible, natural human reactions. However, this field remains in an early stage. We observe a key limitation in current video-driven human reaction generation—video inputs often fail to effectively steer reaction motion generation, leading to human reactions that are severely mismatched with the content of videos. This observation inspires us to ask two natural research questions: RQ1. What limits the effectiveness of videos in steering human reaction synthesis? RQ2. How can we guide the model to produce correct reactions based on video inputs? 

To answer the above questions, we begin by conducting an in-depth anal-ysis, which is summarized in Fig. 2. We find that the key factor limiting the effectiveness of videos in steering human reaction generation lies in a severe rela-tional distortion between visual observations and reaction categories, which disrupts the correspondence between what is observed and how a person should react. For example, as shown in Fig. 2 (a), observation embeddings extracted from video inputs exhibit high off-diagonal correlations across reaction types, reflecting severe inter-category interference. This is also evidenced by the t-SNE visualization in the figure, where the observation embeddings are highly scat-tered and partially overlapped rather than forming compact and well-separated clusters. Such distorted relationships make models confuse interaction intents and produce incorrect human reactions, highlighting the importance of purifying visual observations. Building on our findings for RQ1 , we propose a MuSteerNet framework that addresses RQ2 by leveraging mutual steering between visual observations and reactions. Specifically, on one hand, we introduce a Prototype Feedback Steering (PFS) mechanism, which aims to learn a set of prototypical vectors from human reactions and feeds them back to refine observation embeddings via using a gated delta-rectification modulator constrained by a relational margin penalty. By explicitly correcting relational margins among extracted visual ob-servation embeddings, PFS can effectively mitigate the relational distortion issue and produce more discriminative visual cues. On the other hand, we propose a Dual-Coupled Reaction Refinement (DCRR) scheme, aiming to fully exploit the rectified observation embeddings to, in turn, steer the refinement of human reaction motions. As a result, the quality of reaction motions can be MuSteerNet 3

effectively improved by our observation-reaction mutual steering framework. We conduct extensive experiments to validate our approach. The results show that our MuSteerNet consistently outperforms prior methods, achieving the lowest FID of 0.328 , a diversity score of 7.895 that is closer to real motions, and an improved multimodality score of 1.648 . More importantly, our work offers a new perspective for future research on video-driven reaction synthesis, which is overlooked by the current work. We summarize the main contributions of this paper here: a). We identify the key factor that undermines the effectiveness of video inputs in steering human reaction generation—there exists a severe relational distortion between visual observations and reaction types. b). We propose MuSteerNet, a mutual-steering framework for video-driven human reaction generation. It mitigates the relational distortion issue via prototype feedback steering and enhances reaction quality with dual-coupled reaction refinement, thereby guiding the model to produce correct reaction motions. c). Extensive experiments and ablation studies validate the effectiveness of our approach and demonstrate that it achieves competitive performance. 

# 2 Related Work 

Human Motion Generation. Human motion generation aims to create realistic and expressive 3D human motions conditioned on user-provided instructions. Prior studies employ a wide spectrum of generative paradigms to model human motion sequences. For example, [58,59,61] were built upon GANs [12], [7,21,25,28, 47, 52, 64, 73] utilized diffusion models [18, 48, 76] to achieve high-quality synthesis, and [1, 2, 15, 17, 41, 42] resorted to VAEs [27] to model stochastic relationships between conditions and reaction motions. In addition, [11, 16, 24, 66, 69, 71] formulated human motion generation in an autoregressive manner, whereas masked generative modeling [3] was employed in recent methods [10, 13, 43, 45] to enable high-fidelity human motion synthesis with improved efficiency. While our MuSteerNet also leverages masked generative modeling, it tackles an obviously different task—video-driven human reaction generation—which requires models to perceive interaction intents of given video sequences and generate plausible human reaction motions that directly respond to observed visual content [63]. 

Human Reaction Generation. Human reaction generation requires models to perceive interaction intents from inputs and synthesize reactions that directly respond to them, unlike human motion synthesis, which typically relies on explicit conditioning signals. Existing approaches mainly focus on human-human motion interactions, where the goal is to predict the reactor’s motion solely conditioned on the actor’s motion [6, 9, 33, 60]. Diffusion models have been adopted in [9, 60] to model human-human reactions, [6] proposed fully capturing spatio-temporal dependencies to enhance interaction coherence, and [32] introduced a forward-dynamics-guided imitation strategy for generating physically plausible human reactions. # 3 Relational Distortion Analysis 

In this section, we analyze the relational distortion issue. We first formalize the problem in Proposition 1 and then provide our analysis in detail. 

Proposition 1 (Relational Distortion). Let E = {ei}Ni=1 denote a set of visual observation embeddings, and Y = {yi}Ni=1 represent the categories of reaction motions for {ei}Ni=1 . We say that a relational distortion occurs in E if relations MuSteerNet 5

among observation embeddings are inconsistent with their reaction types, i.e. , the relation score s(ei, ej ) between ei and ej is spuriously high even when yi̸ = yj .The relational distortion issue introduced in Proposition 1 easily results in blurred decision boundaries and an ambiguous correspondence between what is observed and how a person should react . In the following, we provide experimental evidence to analyze the relational distortion problem. 

Diagnostic Analysis. We summarize our analysis of the relational distortion issue observed in [63] in Fig. 2. As can be seen from Fig. 2 (a), the global relations of visual observation embeddings across reaction categories in the baseline [63] are spuriously high, where we use cosine similarity to measure relations between observation embeddings. This indicates that visual observations fail to preserve inter-category discriminability with respect to reaction types, and observation embeddings from distinct reaction categories instead collapse into overly similar visual representations. For example, as shown in the figure, the embeddings of “wave” and “hug” are highly entangled and difficult to distinguish, even though the corresponding reaction motions are markedly different, which misleads the model into generating incorrect human reactions. As illustrated by the failure cases provided in Fig. 2 (a), the model incorrectly responds to a “hug” with a “wave” motion, and conversely reacts to a “wave” with a motion resembling a “hug”. This finding is further supported by the t-SNE visualization in the figure, which shows that observation embeddings are highly scattered and partially overlapping, rather than forming compact, well-separated clusters. Our analysis addresses RQ1 and sheds light on why video inputs often fail to effectively steer reaction generation. Solving the relational distortion issue is crucial for generating high-quality hu-man reaction motions, as it helps the model to establish an accurate mapping from observed visual cues to the categories of reactions. To this end, in Proposition 2 ,we point out the importance of conducting observation purification. 

Proposition 2 (Observation Purification). We argue that observation pu-rification is crucial for improving video-driven human reaction generation. We formalize it as suppressing spurious relations between observation embeddings; specifically, for any pair with yi̸ = yj , the relation score s(ei, ej ) is suppressed to a low value, thereby restoring clear decision boundaries among reaction categories in E and yielding a more faithful depiction of observation-reaction correspondence. In Fig. 2 (b), we also visualize the visual observation embeddings after rectification by our observation-reaction mutual steering framework. The rectified embeddings become more discriminative with respect to reaction categories, which in turn helps the model generate more coherent human reactions. For example, the model is guided to produce correct reactions to “hug” and “wave”, rather than confusing these two inputs. Remark 2. Differences from [63] in Training the Residual Transformer. 

The key differences between [63] and our method in training the residual Trans-former lie in the use of the dual-coupled refinement strategy. First, we jointly leverage original and rectified observation embeddings to guide training of the residual Transformer, whereas [63] relies solely on original visual observations. This enables our method to preserve original visual cues while benefiting from the guidance of rectified embeddings. Second, [63] trains the residual Transformer 

TR by supervising it with discrete motion tokens extracted from ground-truth reactions paired with training videos. In contrast, our method trains TR via explicitly considering the predictions drawn by the motion Transformer TM .Thereby, TR can be guided to jointly consider hierarchical residual motion tokens encoded by RVQ-VAE and prediction errors induced by TM , encouraging the residual Transformer TR to better refine human reactions produced by TM .

Remark 3. Key Insights and Takeaways. (1) We identify a key bottleneck in current video-driven human reaction generation—the relational distortion between visual observations and reaction types. Our work not only clarifies why existing methods struggle to steer reaction synthesis using videos, but also opens a principled direction for future research: enhancing video-driven reaction generation via further purifying visual cues extracted from video sequences. (2) Going beyond [63], we move beyond the one-way pipeline and reformulate the training of reaction synthesis models as an observation-reaction mutual-steering framework, establishing a new baseline for future research.

We first give the experimental settings in Section 5.1, and then provide the main results and the analysis of MuSteerNet in Section 5.2 and 5.3. For additional experiments and results, please refer to our supplementary material. MuSteerNet 11 

5.1 Experimental Settings Benchmark. Following [63], we evaluate our approach on the ViMo dataset. It includes 3500 videos, and each video is manually paired with a plausible reaction motion, forming 3500 video-motion pairs across 26 reaction categories. We adopt the same data split as [63] using 2800 samples for training and 700 samples for testing. For evaluation, we follow [63], and report metrics widely used in human motion generation [15, 17, 60], including Fréchet Inception Distance (FID), diversity, and multimodality. All results are averaged over 20 trials. FID serves as the primary evaluation metric for overall generation quality. We compute it as the Fréchet distance between the feature distributions of generated reaction motions and ground-truth human reactions, using the same evaluation protocol as in [63] and the motion feature extractor from HumanML3D [15]. In addition, multimodality measures how much generated reactions diversify within each category, and diversity quantifies the overall variance across reaction types. 

Compared Baselines. HERO [63] is the primary compared baseline in our experiments. Since video-driven reaction generation is a new task introduced by [63], we follow its official benchmark and additionally report the results of the open-source methods [5, 14, 44, 51, 67]. These results are taken directly from the official paper of [63], where the authors have adapted these approaches to this new task. All results are obtained under the same evaluation protocol as [63]. 

Implementation Details. We implement our method in PyTorch and run all experiments on an A6000 GPU card. Following [63], we initialize the motion RVQ-VAE with weights pretrained on HumanML3D [15], and then finetune it on ViMo for 10 epochs using AdamW [34] with a learning rate of 2e −4 and a batch size of 256 . We set the number of the RVQ-VAE’s quantization layers as 6, and use the same Transformer architectures adopted in [63] to build our Transformer models. Following [63], we adopt TC-CLIP [26] as our video encoder and train the motion and residual Transformers for 80 and 40 epochs respectively, using the AdamW optimizer with a weight decay of 1e −5, a learning rate of 5e −4, and a batch size of 32 . Also, the hyperparameters μ and ε are set as 30 and 0.4.

5.3 Method Analysis Ablation Study of Prototype Feedback Steering. We provide an ablation study of our Prototype Feedback Steering (PFS) in Table 2, where DCRR is discarded from MuSteerNet to better isolate the effect of PFS. The results indicate that using PFS yields a clear improvement in reaction quality; for example, it reduces FID from 0.493 (“w/o PFS”) to 0.366 (“w/ PFS”). We ablate the key components of our gated delta-rectification modulator used to purify visual observations. Removing the delta term (“w/o Delta”), gate (“w/o Gate”), or linear layers (“w/o Linear”) consistently degrades performance, increasing FID to 0.462 , 0.422 , and 0.469 , respectively. These results validate our design for delta rectification and dynamic gating, which are beneficial for further refining observation embeddings. Fig. 2 also qualitatively validates the effectiveness of PFS in alleviating the relational distortion issue. As can be seen from the figure, PFS effectively reduces spurious correlations between observation embeddings and reaction types, and rectifies the embeddings to form compact, well-separated clusters that guide the model to generate correct reaction motions. Thus, RQ2 

can be addressed, and the importance of observation purification is validated. We also study the base Transformer and find that using RVQ-VAE to build prototypes (“w/o BTrans”) causes a clear FID drop, consistent with the results in Fig. 6, where the base Transformer accelerates the convergence of LRMC to a lower value and yields motion embeddings with better inter-class separability. 

Ablation Study of Dual-Coupled Reaction Refinement. We evaluate the effectiveness of our Dual-Coupled Reaction Refinement (DCRR) strategy in Table 3. From the table, we have the following observations. First, utilizing DCRR (“w/ DCRR”) consistently improves our method’s overall performance; for instance, it reduces FID from 0.366 to 0.328 , improves multimodality from 1.198 

to 1.648 , and brings motion diversity closer to that of real motions. These results indicate that our DCRR not only boosts the fidelity of generated reactions but also enhances their diversity. Second, we also ablate the key components of TwinMixer MuSteerNet 15 

and find that removing either the original branch (“w/o OBranch”) or the rectified branch (“w/o RBranch”) leads to a clear performance degradation. For instance, FID increases to 0.349 when using only original observation embeddings and to 

0.375 when retaining only rectified observations. The full TwinMixer obviously achieves the best multimodality score and a diversity score closer to that of real human reactions. 

# 6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we study video-driven human reaction generation and identify the relational distortion between visual observations and reaction types as a key obstacle in bridging what is observed and how a person should react. To address this issue, we propose a MuSteerNet framework based on observation-reaction mutual steering: Prototype Feedback Steering rectifies observation embeddings, while Dual-Coupled Reaction Refinement leverages rectified cues to further refine reaction motions. Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of our method and show consistent improvements in reaction quality. We plan to extend our mutual-steering framework to more conditioning signals (e.g., audio) and more challenging settings (e.g., multi-person interaction) in future work.

# B Pose Representation 

We employ the same pose representation as in [15] for representing reaction motions, which is expressive, well suited to neural networks, and has been widely used by recent motion generation approaches. Specifically, each pose is represented as ( ˙ra, ˙rx, ˙rz , ry , jp, jv , jr , cf ), which consists of three main components: (1) root dynamics, including the yaw angular velocity ˙ra, the planar linear velocities 

( ˙rx, ˙rz ), and the root height ry ; (2) per-joint kinematic features in the root coordinate system, namely joint positions jp ∈ R3j , joint velocities jv ∈ R3j ,and 6D joint rotations jr ∈ R6j for j joints; and (3) a 4-dimensional binary foot-contact indicator cf ∈ R4, obtained by thresholding the heel and toe velocities. 

# C Evaluation Metrics 

In this section, we elaborate on the evaluation metrics employed in our work, including Fréchet Inception Distance (FID), diversity, and multimodality. We use FID as the primary metric to evaluate the quality of generated human reactions. It is computed as the Fréchet distance between the feature distributions of generated reaction motions and ground-truth human reactions, where we adopt the same feature extractor as in [15] and follow the evaluation protocol adopted in [63]. We measure the diversity score by averaging the Euclidean distances between 300 randomly sampled pairs of generated reactions, following the same procedure as in [63]. Multimodality aims to evaluate the variation among multiple motions generated from the same video. For each video, we first sample 10 pairs of generated motions, compute their average pairwise Euclidean distance, and then average the scores across videos to obtain the final multimodality score. We follow the previous work [63] by repeating each experiment 20 times and reporting the mean with the 95% confidence interval. For more details on these three widely-used metrics, we refer readers to [15, 17, 63].

L.3 Gated Delta-Rectification Modulator 

The gated delta-rectification modulator is designed to rectify extracted visual observation embeddings via an adaptive gated residual transformation. We show its architecture details in Algorithm 3, including five components: a normalization layer, a first linear projection layer, a GELU activation layer, a second linear pro-jection layer, and a gate branch. TwinMixer is designed to fuse input features coming from different sources through an affine transformation, whose architecture details are shown in Algorithm 4. Our TwinMixer consists of two main components: a two-layer MLP and an affine fusion operation. These two factors are then applied to another input feature x2 through an affine operation to obtain fused output y. We set “ in_dim = 512 ”, “ cond_dim = 512 ”, and “ hidden_dim = 512 ”. 

# M Broader Impacts 

Vision plays a crucial role in how humans perceive and understand the world. Prior studies have shown that visual information constitutes the majority of human sensory input, far exceeding other modalities [23, 46]. Compared with textual descriptions, videos encode richer environmental cues and more com-plex interaction dynamics. From this perspective, our work is not merely about generating human motions according to specific instructions, but about advanc-ing machines toward a more active understanding of how to respond to the surrounding world through visual observation. We believe this capability holds broad application potential, particularly in the rapidly growing area of embodied intelligence. At the same time, we acknowledge that such technology may also introduce potential risks, including misuse in behavior simulation, misleading virtual human generation, or biased reaction modeling caused by limitations and imbalances in training data. These concerns highlight the importance of responsible development, careful dataset design, and appropriate safeguards for future research and deployment. 

# N Limitations and Future Work 

We note that video-driven reaction generation is still at an early stage. The limitations of our work, and even of the whole community in this field, are threefold. First, due to the difficulty of collecting paired data, a high-quality, large-scale dataset for this task is still lacking. Although a recent work [68] MuSteerNet 35 

has begun to explore this direction, its dataset remains relatively small in scale and has not yet been publicly released. Second, existing methods mainly focus on short-term reaction synthesis, while long-horizon reasoning over intention, context, and interaction dynamics remains underexplored. Third, video encoders specifically tailored for video-driven reaction generation are still unavailable and are not explicitly aligned with reaction motions or interaction dynamics. In future work, we plan to move forward in three directions: constructing a high-quality, large-scale benchmark dataset, extending our framework to long-horizon reaction generation scenarios, and developing a dedicated video encoder for human reaction generation instead of relying solely on lightweight adapters.
