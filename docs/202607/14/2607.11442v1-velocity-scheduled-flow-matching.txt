# ABSTRACT 

Flow matching trains a neural network to regress the conditional velocity along a linear interpolant between noise and data, and the number of network evaluations (NFE) sets the cost of sampling. The straight-line interpolant carries an implicit choice: the sample moves at constant speed throughout the trajectory. We relax this choice and introduce Velocity Scheduled Flow Matching (VSFM), which replaces the conditional target x1 −x0 with v(t)( x1 −x0) for any nonnegative profile v : [0 , 1] → R≥0

satisfying R 10 v dt = 1 . We study six polynomial profiles drawn from motion planning. The first use of VSFM is at inference time: a pretrained linear flow-matching model can be sampled under any admissible profile by integrating its ODE on a non-uniform τ -schedule, with no retraining and no additional computation; on CIFAR-10 this lowers FID by up to 19 .8% . Training from scratch under a braking profile gives a further reduction of 17 .4% at 4 NFE. Both gains follow from the local truncation error of the Euler integrator on the induced grid. 

Keywords Flow matching · Generative models · Velocity profile · Machine learning · Image generation · Ordinary differential equations · Local truncation error · Training-free adaptation. 

# 1 Introduction 

Flow matching is a continuous-time generative modeling framework with applications in image synthesis [ 1, 2, 3]and scientific simulation [ 4, 5]. A neural velocity field vθ (x, t ) is trained to match the conditional velocity along an interpolant between noise x0 ∼ p0 and data x1 ∼ p1, and samples are drawn by integrating the learned ODE 

dx/dt = vθ (x, t ), so that the trained model realizes a learned transformation from the noise distribution to the data distribution [ 6 ]. The sampling cost is set by the number of network evaluations (NFE) needed to integrate the ODE accurately. This NFE budget is the inference-time compute that can be traded for sample quality, an instance of inference-time scaling [ 7 ]. The low-NFE regime, in which a handful of Euler steps must carry the trajectory from noise to data, is the active frontier for fast samplers built on pretrained models, including large-scale text-to-image systems [8, 9]. Existing flow-matching models fix the interpolant to the straight line xt = x0 + t(x1 − x0). The choice is convenient: the conditional velocity reduces to the time-independent displacement x1 − x0, probability-flow trajectories are straight and admit an optimal-transport interpretation [ 1, 10 ]. It also commits the sampler to moving at constant speed from noise to data, a property that prior work has not isolated as a design choice. We generalize the conditional target from a constant to a time-varying form, 

ut(x | x0, x 1) = v(t) · (x1 − x0), (1) where v : [0 , 1] → R≥0 is a velocity profile satisfying R 10 v(t) dt = 1 . The unit integral preserves total displacement, so every admissible v transports noise to data; the remaining freedom is the rate at which the sample moves at each instant. Setting v(t) ≡ 1 recovers standard flow matching. We call the resulting framework Velocity Scheduled Flow Matching 

(VSFM); the architecture, loss, coupling, and integrator are otherwise unchanged. Section 3.2 instantiates VSFM with six polynomial profiles drawn from boundary-value problems in motion planning. Two consequences follow from this generalization. The first is a training-free inference-time rescheduling: any admissible profile can be applied to a pretrained linear flow-matching model by running its ODE on the non-uniform arXiv:2607.11442v1 [cs.LG] 13 Jul 2026 Bondar V. Velocity Scheduled Flow Matching step schedule that the profile induces (Section 3.3). The second is training from scratch under a non-constant profile, which amounts to a one-line modification of the standard loss and yields additional FID gains, with the largest effect at low NFE. On CIFAR-10, training from scratch under the coasting profile lowers FID by 17.4% at 4 NFE and 10.4% at 50 NFE rela-tive to the linear baseline. Applied as an inference-time modification to the publicly available TorchCFM checkpoint [ 3], the same profile lowers FID by up to 19.8% across the NFE budgets we evaluated, without retraining. Section 3.4 traces both effects to the local truncation error of the Euler sampler in the τ -space ODE. The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 positions VSFM against prior work. Section 3 develops the training target, derives the inference-time rescheduling algorithm, and analyses the discretization error it induces. Section 4 reports the CIFAR-10 experiments, and Section 5 concludes and outlines directions for follow-up work. 

# 2 Related Work 

VSFM connects to four threads of prior work: the choice of interpolant in flow matching, kinetic-energy analyses of probability paths, noise schedules and loss weighting in diffusion, and time-sampling strategies at training and inference. 

2.1 Flow matching and the linear interpolant 

Flow matching was introduced by Lipman et al. [ 1] and Liu et al. [ 2] and extended to arbitrary couplings by Tong et al. [ 3]. In all three formulations the conditional interpolant is the straight line xt = x0 + t(x1 − x0), a choice retained by most subsequent work, including the rectified-flow transformer underlying Stable Diffusion 3 [ 9]. Rectified flow [ 2]straightens the learned flow by iterative re-coupling; Consistency Flow Matching [ 11 ] enforces a self-consistency constraint on the velocity field; both stay within the linear interpolant. SplineFlow [ 12 ] replaces the linear interpolant with a B-spline construction in a multi-marginal dynamical-systems setting, targeting different goals and offering no inference-time adaptation for pretrained models. Albergo and Vanden-Eijnden [ 13 ] and Pooladian et al. [ 14 ]study stochastic interpolants and multisample flow matching at the level of theory; both modify the coupling and the probability path, while VSFM alters the speed at which the trajectory is traversed. VSFM also changes the interpolant but does so for two-marginal generative modeling, uses a simpler polynomial family, and admits a training-free inference variant. 

2.2 Kinetic-energy perspectives on flow matching 

Shaul et al. [ 10 ] identify the Gaussian probability path that minimizes kinetic energy R 10 E[∥ut∥2] dt within a class of affine conditionals, and show that the straight-line constant-velocity path is approximately optimal under that criterion. VSFM optimizes a different quantity, namely the local truncation error of the Euler integrator at a fixed step budget. That criterion depends on how discretization error is distributed along t, rather than on integrated squared velocity, and Section 3.4 demonstrates that a non-constant profile is preferred. The two results are compatible: constant velocity minimizes kinetic energy, while a non-constant velocity lowers Euler error at fixed NFE. Neither the kinetic-energy formulation nor the rectified-flow literature provides a way to adapt the sampling dynamics of an already-trained model; Section 3.3 supplies one. 

2.3 Noise schedules and loss weighting in diffusion 

The design of the forward process in diffusion models has long been recognized as influential on sample quality. Sohl-Dickstein et al. [ 15 ] introduced the diffusion framework; Ho et al. [ 16 ] scaled it with a tractable denoising objective and a linear variance schedule. Song et al. [ 17 ] unified diffusion and score-based models as SDEs, placing the ODE-based inference used by flow matching in a broader probabilistic setting. Dhariwal and Nichol [ 18 ] showed that classifier guidance enables diffusion models to reach high sample quality on class-conditional benchmarks. Nichol and Dhariwal [ 19 ] proposed a cosine schedule that avoids degeneracy near the pure-noise endpoint. Karras et al. [ 20 ]separated and independently tuned the forward process, the sampler, and the training components. P2 weighting [ 21 ]and Min-SNR weighting [ 22 ] reweight the per-noise-level training loss, and Kingma and Gao [ 23 ] give a unifying likelihood interpretation of such weighted losses. VSFM modifies the interpolant rather than the schedule or a loss coefficient, and, unlike loss-weighting methods, admits an inference-time variant that requires no retraining. 2Bondar V. 2.4 Time sampling and step allocation 

A separate line of work alters which times t are sampled during training, or the discretization grid at inference, while leaving the linear interpolant fixed. Esser et al. [ 9 ] bias training-time sampling toward the center of [0 , 1] through a logit-normal distribution. Karras et al. [ 20 ] design non-uniform inference-time grids for diffusion ODEs. Dense-Jump Flow Matching [ 24 ] combines U-shaped training-time sampling with a jump-based solver for robotic policies. All three perturb p(t) or the discretization grid while keeping the interpolant linear. The inference procedure of Section 3.3 reduces, in mechanical terms, to running the pretrained sampler on the non-uniform schedule τi = α(i/N ), and therefore belongs to this family. What VSFM adds is a structured parameterization of the schedule through the velocity profile v(t), a training-time variant that aligns the model with its inference schedule, and a discretization-error account (Section 3.4) that singles out the braking sub-family without a grid search. 

2.5 Polynomial movement profiles in motion planning 

Polynomial movement profiles are standard tools in robotics, animation, and CNC machining for generating smooth actuator paths under boundary conditions on velocity and acceleration [ 25 ]. Flash and Hogan [ 26 ] showed that the minimum-jerk profile uniquely minimizes integrated squared jerk subject to zero velocity and acceleration at both endpoints, and it has remained a standard model of unconstrained human reaching movements ever since. These kinematic profiles have not previously been connected to generative modeling.

Summary. A velocity profile that places small v(ti)2, and therefore small (∆ τi)2, where the learned field vθ varies most rapidly lowers the local Euler truncation error relative to the linear baseline. Among the six profiles, the braking sub-family has this structure with respect to τ = 1 and the symmetric sub-family does not. The corresponding prediction is that braking profiles improve over the baseline, with the largest gain at low NFE, where truncation error dominates, and shrinking gains at high NFE, where it does not. The experiments of Section 4 confirm this pattern. The picture changes when the source x0 is itself structured, as in image-to-image tasks: field variation near τ = 0 becomes significant, and the bilateral suppression of the symmetric profiles becomes useful.

Coasting attains the best FID at every NFE budget: 35 .39 at 4 NFE ( −17 .4% over linear), 17 .12 at 8 NFE ( −15 .9% ), and 8.29 at 50 NFE ( −10 .4% ). 4.4 Analysis Which profiles help and why The braking sub-family exhibits the same pattern in both experimental settings: profiles with monotonically decreasing velocity improve on linear, the sole exception being smooth landing (treated below), while the symmetric profiles deliver only marginal gains. This is the prediction of the step-size analysis in Table 2. Braking profiles assign small ∆τi near t = 1 , the data manifold, where the velocity field vθ (x, τ ) is most nonlinear, and so suppress the dominant contribution to Euler truncation error. 

The smooth-landing exception Smooth landing trails linear in both training (every NFE) and inference (32 NFE). Its high initial velocity v(0) = 3 allocates the largest τ -steps to the near-noise portion of the trajectory, where the field is relatively smooth, and leaves only a handful of small steps for t ≈ 1, where precision matters most. At 32 NFE the budget spent on the initial steps is wasted; the profile becomes beneficial only once the step count is large enough that those final small steps dominate the error. Coasting, with the milder v(0) = 3 /2, decelerates more gently and strikes a better balance, which is why it ranks first across all settings tested. 

# 5 Conclusions 

This paper introduced Velocity Scheduled Flow Matching (VSFM), a generalization of flow matching in which the constant conditional velocity target x1 − x0 is replaced by the time-varying form v(t)( x1 − x0) for any nonnegative profile v with R 10 v dt = 1 . The framework supports two distinct uses. At inference time, integrating the pretrained 10 Bondar V. Velocity Scheduled Flow Matching model’s τ -space ODE (Equation (8) ) with exact steps ∆τi = α(ti+1 ) − α(ti) applies any admissible velocity profile to an existing linear flow-matching model with no retraining; the coasting profile lowers FID by up to 19 .8% on CIFAR-10. At training time, fitting the network under a non-constant profile delivers a further drop in FID of 17 .4% at 4 NFE and 

10 .4% at 50 NFE relative to the linear baseline. A single mechanism underlies both effects: braking profiles damp the field-variation term (∆ τi)2 · ∂2x/∂τ 2 of the Euler local truncation error near τ = 1 , the region where the learned velocity field varies most rapidly. Taken together, these results identify the velocity profile as a simple and low-cost lever for improving few-step sampling in flow matching, and the present study, conducted on CIFAR-10 ( 32 ×32 ) with six polynomial profiles under a fixed-step Euler sampler, opens several directions for further work. Replication on higher-resolution and more diverse image datasets would establish how broadly the observed gains extend. The discretization-error analysis developed here is a local argument; a global convergence bound relating the properties of a profile to the total integration error would place the mechanism on a firmer theoretical footing. The profile family can be broadened beyond the polynomial case to trigonometric and sigmoidal forms, or learned jointly with the network weights instead of being fixed in advance. The interaction between velocity scheduling and higher-order solvers such as DPM-Solver [ 29 ] and DDIM [ 30 ], which cancel the leading truncation error, also merits study, as it would clarify the regimes in which the choice of profile contributes most. VSFM schedules could further be combined with progressive distillation [31] or consistency training to reduce sampling cost. Finally, velocity profiles may transfer to scientific generation tasks such as molecular dynamics [ 4 ] and protein structure prediction [ 5], where the structure of the data manifold differs from that of natural images.
