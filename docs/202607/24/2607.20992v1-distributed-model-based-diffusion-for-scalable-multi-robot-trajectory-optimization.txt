# Distributed Model-Based Diffusion For Scalable Multi-Robot Trajectory Optimization 

Haejoon Lee, Xinyi Wang, Taekyung Kim, and Dimitra Panagou 

Abstract — Trajectory optimization for multi-robot systems remains a critical challenge, particularly when navigating highly non-convex, non-linear, and non-differentiable envi-ronments. While Model-Based Diffusion (MBD) has recently emerged as a promising sampling-based optimization paradigm for single-robot trajectory generation, extending it to multi-robot systems results in a centralized, high-dimensional infer-ence problem that (i) suffers from poor sample efficiency due to the curse of dimensionality and (ii) requires global access to all robots’ dynamics, constraints, and objectives. To address this, we propose Distributed Model-Based Diffusion (DMBD), a distributed server-robot framework that decomposes the reverse diffusion process into local conditional reverse diffusion processes. This decomposition enables each robot to iteratively perform denoising independently within its own control sub-space while conditioning on the current trajectory estimates of the other robots that are aggregated and broadcast by the server. Extensive simulations in goal swapping, multi-floor coverage, parking, and rush-hour scenarios demonstrate that DMBD achieves strong scalability, solving many challenging co-ordination tasks in sub-seconds and significantly outperforming existing baselines. I. I NTRODUCTION 

Trajectory optimization for collision-free multi-robot co-ordination within shared workspaces remains a critical chal-lenge. One of the most popular approaches is the gradient-based methods that use gradient of the cost function to move toward the optimal solutions [1]–[4]. However, these meth-ods often struggle in highly nonconvex or non-differentiable settings, where they are susceptible to poor local minima and can incur substantial computational costs. To circumvent the limitations of gradient-based optimiza-tion methods, sampling-based optimization (SBO) methods have emerged as a powerful alternative. By casting the trajectory planning problem as a probabilistic inference prob-lem over the trajectory space, SBO planners have proven highly successful at navigating complex, non-smooth, and non-convex environments [5]–[8]. Representative examples include the Cross Entropy Method (CEM) [5], [9], Model Predictive Path Integral (MPPI) [6], [10], and variational MPCs [11], [12]. Recently, Model-Based Diffusion (MBD) has emerged as a promising SBO method inspired by generative denoising diffusion probabilistic models [7], [13]. While algorithmi-cally similar, it departs from other SBO approaches, as MBD leverages the diffusion framework to optimize over a multi-modal trajectory distribution via iterative denoising

process. Furthermore, in contrast to other diffusion-based planners that rely on score networks learned from offline datasets [14]–[16], MBD directly exploits known system dynamics and task objectives during inference, enabling 

learning-free generation of diverse, low-cost trajectories. Since its introduction, several extensions of MBD have been proposed. For example, gradual constraint enforcement was introduced in [17] to progressively improve solution quality throughout the denoising process, while strict safety guarantees were incorporated directly into the denoising pro-cedure in [18]. Related diffusion-inspired denoising schemes are adopted in [8] to present a real-time controller. MBD’s ability to optimize over multi-modal trajectory distributions is appealing for multi-robot planning, where deadlocks often arise from local minima. However, despite its success in single-agent settings, extending MBD to multi-robot systems remains challenging for two reasons. First, MBD suffers from the curse of dimensionality as the number of robots increases. Because the dimension of the joint trajectory space grows with the number of robots [19], MBD, like other SBO methods, experiences exponentially decreasing sampling efficiency and optimization performance in large-scale multi-robot settings. D4orm [20] mitigates this issue through iterative deformation updates, but its reverse diffusion process remains centralized, requiring a single processor to sample and denoise trajectories in the joint trajectory space. A recent work [21] combines individu-ally executed MBD planning with centralized deconfliction, which may require numerous rounds of deconfliction and subsequent MBD replanning in congested environments. Second, MBD assumes centralized access to all objec-tives, constraints, and robot dynamics to perform denoising. In practical multi-robot systems, particularly heterogeneous teams, robots often possess distinct objectives, dynamics, and local constraints. Aggregating this information can incur substantial communication overhead and places a significant computational and memory burden on a single machine, further limiting scalability. To address these challenges, this paper introduces Dis-tributed Model-Based Diffusion (DMBD), a distributed, server-robot framework for multi-robot trajectory optimiza-tion, reflecting deployment architectures commonly found in warehouse and delivery systems [22], [23]. DMBD de-composes the global trajectory optimization problem into local inference subproblems executed independently and in parallel by each robot. Rather than estimating the score function and performing denoising over the full joint tra-jectory distribution, each robot performs local conditional arXiv:2607.20992v1 [cs.RO] 23 Jul 2026 denoising updates using only its own objectives, constraints, and dynamics. During each denoising step, it conditions on the current trajectory estimates of the other robots, which are aggregated and broadcast by the server. In contrast to existing distributed sampling-based ap-proaches [11], [24], which require agents to exchange mul-tiple trajectory samples and therefore incur substantial com-munication overhead, DMBD communicates only a single trajectory per denoising iteration. Compared to [25], our method does not require robots to know other robots’ lo-cal objective functions. Furthermore, unlike gradient-based approaches such as [26], DMBD is entirely zeroth-order and does not assume differentiability. Consequently, DMBD achieves scalable multi-robot coordination while retaining the efficiency and multi-modal trajectory generation of MBD. Our contributions are as follows:  

> •

We propose Distributed Model-Based Diffusion (DMBD) , a server-coordinated distributed variant of MBD that decomposes multi-robot trajectory optimiza-tion into local conditional diffusion processes. Each robot independently performs denoising in its own trajectory space while conditioning on the trajectories of other robots, reducing inference complexity and improving scalability and sample efficiency.  

> •

We characterize the relationship between MBD and DMBD by deriving a theoretical bound on the discrep-ancy between the global score function and the local conditional score function.  

> •

We validate DMBD through extensive simulations across diverse multi-robot scenarios, including goal swapping, multi-floor coverage, parking, and rush-hour tasks, demonstrating superior scalability and consistent performance gains over existing baselines. II. P RELIMINARIES

D ISTRIBUTED MODEL -B ASED DIFFUSION (DMBD) While MBD provides an efficient mechanism for sampling low-cost trajectories under p0, it has two key limitations. First, it assumes access to the global objective J, global dynamics F , and all constraint functions gk,p . In a multi-robot setting, however, each local objective Jk, dynamics fk,and constraints gk,p may only be available to robot k ∈ R .Second, MBD scales poorly with the number of robots, since the dimensionality of the joint control space grows with N ,reducing sampling efficiency and thus performance. To address these issues, we propose Distributed Model-Based Diffusion (DMBD), a server-robot framework that de-composes the joint reverse diffusion process into distributed conditional denoising processes. DMBD shares the same noising process as vanilla MBD but differs in its denoising process. At each denoising step, robot k locally updates its control trajectory Yk ∈ UTk using its local objective, dynamics, and constraints, while conditioning on the current state trajectory estimates of other robots aggregated and broadcast by the server. Each robot then transmits its updated state trajectory back to the server, which aggregates and rebroadcasts them for the next step (see Figure 1). Fig. 1. Combining (23) with (25), we get (19). Proposition 1 characterizes the discrepancy between the centralized score and local conditional score functions at each denoising step. The bound depends on (i) the deviation of other robots’ optimal trajectories from their current esti-mates and (ii) the Lipschitz constant Hk, which measures the sensitivity of robot k’s denoiser to other robots’ trajectories. One thing to note is that when the constraints are fully decoupled, i.e., gk,p depends only on robot k’s state trajectory (Rk,p = {k}), the denoiser is independent of τ−k and thus Hk = 0 . Consequently, when all robots run DMBD without any coupled constraints, that is equivalent to robots individually running MBD, as expected. 

Remark 3. The bound in Proposition 1 is stated for the exact score functions. In practice, however, both MBD and DMBD estimate these scores using finite sets of samples (as in (11) and (17)). Consequently, (19) characterizes the asymptotic behavior as the number of particles satisfies

In this section, we present simulation results to evaluate our method. Specifically, we aim to address two questions: 

Q1. Is DMBD scalable with respect to the number of robots

3) Results and Discussions : As shown in Fig. 3, DMBD generally achieves the best performance in both success rate and planning time as N increases. DMBD solves goal-swapping tasks in sub-second runtimes even for large teams. In contrast, baselines suffer from rapidly increasing planning time and decreasing success rate. D4orm performs comparably to DMBD in the circular-robot goal-swapping scenario but requires approximately 10 × longer planning time. They also eventually fail at N = 20 due to out-of-memory (OOM) errors when sampling from the high-dimensional joint trajectory space. This scalability advantage arises because baseline methods optimize over the coupled trajectory space of dimension 

(PNk=1 mk)T , which grows with team size, whereas DMBD decomposes inference into per-robot trajectory spaces of di-mension mkT . Furthermore, the performance gap widens as task complexity increases, from circular robots to rectangular robots and multi-floor coverage, where rectangular-body col-lision constraints and elevator dynamics introduce additional non-smoothness. These results highlight the advantage of DMBD for challenging coordination problems.

The resulting coordination emerges without sharing the global ob-jectives, demonstrating that distributed inference is sufficient to recover globally coordinated behavior. V. C ONCLUSIONS 

We present Distributed Model-Based Diffusion (DMBD), a scalable server-robot framework for multi-robot trajectory optimization. By distributing the reverse diffusion process, DMBD enables each robot to perform local conditional denoising using only local information while conditioning on the trajectory estimates from other robots. We characterize the relationship between the global and local conditional denoising updates by deriving theoretical error bounds.
