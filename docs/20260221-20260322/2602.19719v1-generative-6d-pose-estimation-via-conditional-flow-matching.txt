## GENERATIVE 6D POSE ESTIMATION VIA CONDITIONAL FLOW MATCHING

ABSTRACT 

Existing methods for instance-level 6D pose estimation typi-cally rely on neural networks that either directly regress the pose in SE(3) or estimate it indirectly via local feature match-ing. The former struggle with object symmetries, while the latter fail in the absence of distinctive local features. To over-come these limitations, we propose a novel formulation of 6D pose estimation as a conditional flow matching problem in R3. We introduce Flose, a generative method that infers object poses via a denoising process conditioned on local features. While prior approaches based on conditional flow matching perform denoising solely based on geometric guid-ance, Flose integrates appearance-based semantic features to mitigate ambiguities caused by object symmetries. We further incorporate RANSAC-based registration to handle outliers. We validate Flose on five datasets from the established BOP benchmark. Flose outperforms prior methods with an aver-age improvement of +4.5 Average Recall. Project Website : https://tev-fbk.github.io/Flose/ 

Index Terms — 6D pose estimation, Conditional flow matching, Robotics vision 

1. INTRODUCTION 

While humans interact seamlessly with objects in their envi-ronments, this ability remains a fundamental challenge for robotic agents. Object 6D pose estimation aims to bridge this gap by determining an object’s position and orientation in 3D space from sensory images. This is fundamental for robotic manipulation [1] and augmented reality [2]. In recent years, the field has transitioned from hand-crafted to learning-based methods, which now achieve state-of-the-art performance [ 3, 4 ]. In this work, we focus on learning-based methods that operate under the model-based instance-level 

setting, and use RGBD data [ 5 , 6 , 7 , 3 ]. Model-based methods assume access to a 3D representation of the object, provided as either a synthetic CAD model [ 8 ] or a reconstructed 3D mesh [ 9, 10 , 11 , 12 ]. Instance-level methods rely on direct supervision over a closed set of predefined object instances. These methods can be roughly categorized as direct or indi-rect . The former [ 3 , 13 , 14 , 7] use neural networks to regress the object pose in an end-to-end fashion and are typically trained by minimizing a loss over the SE(3) manifold. They may underperform in the case of object symmetries and suffer from lower accuracy due to the lack of explicit pixel-to-3D alignment. The latter [ 4, 15 , 16 ] extract local features to estab-lish object-image correspondences and infer the object pose via robust registration. These methods may fail to establish reliable correspondences in the absence of distinctive local features [ 17 ]. Recently, generative modeling has emerged as a promising alternative for 3D point cloud registration, reformu-lating registration as a progressive denoising process instead of explicit correspondence estimation [18, 19, 20]. In this work, we formulate 6D pose estimation as a Con-ditional Flow Matching (CFM) [ 21 ] problem in R3 [ 18 ] and present Flose ( Flo w matching for 6D po se estimation) that operates in the model-based instance-level setting. Flose esti-mates the object pose via a denoising process that learns the displacement field required to register noise samples with the object’s 3D model. Prior CFM-based methods suffer from two main limitations. As they condition the denoising process solely on geometric guidance, it may be challenging to resolve 6D pose ambiguities in symmetric shapes where texture pro-vides the only disambiguating cue [ 18 ]. Then, as they typically recover the object pose via global alignment (e.g., SVD [ 22 ]) between input sensory data and the denoised points, they can be sensitive to displacement outliers [ 20 , 18 ]. We address these issues by explicitly capturing object appearance and resolving pose ambiguities arising from symmetries by injecting seman-tic features through a vision foundation model [ 23 ]. Then, we improve robustness to noisy correspondences and displace-ment outliers by replacing global alignment with a random sampling approach [ 24 ], which identifies a subset of points ge-ometrically consistent with a rigid transformation, performing registration exclusively on them. We evaluate Flose on five datasets from the BOP Benchmark [ 9, 8, 10 , 11 , 12 ], spanning diverse objects and real-world conditions. We compare Flose against both direct [ 3, 7 ] and indirect [ 4, 15 , 25 , 6, 16 , 26 ]methods. Compared to the leading method [ 15 ] training a model per dataset, Flose achieves +4.5 Average Recall (AR). Against the leading method [ 3] training a model per object, Flose achieves +1.2 AR while requiring reduced training and inference costs. In summary, our contributions are: • We propose Flose, the first conditional flow matching for-mulation for instance-level object 6D pose estimation. • We integrate semantic features from vision foundation mod-els to disambiguate object symmetries and occlusions. • We employ robust RANSAC-based registration to effectively

2. RELATED WORK Instance-level methods for 6D pose estimation regress object poses from a closed set of predefined instances and can be cat-egorized into direct and indirect approaches. Indirect methods first establish image-object correspondences and then recover the pose using Perspective-n-Point (PnP) or RANSAC-based algorithms. SurfEmb [ 25 ] establishes dense image-object correspondences via surface embeddings. ZebraPose [ 26 ]introduces a hierarchical encoding of the visible object sur-face, while HccePose(BF) [ 4] extends this encoding to in-clude the back surfaces in order to obtain denser correspon-dences. Other indirect approaches focus on refining coarse pose predictions by enforcing consistency across objects or views [ 27 , 7 , 15 ]. These methods remain constrained by the correspondence quality. In contrast, direct methods predict an object’s 6D pose in an end-to-end manner by minimiz-ing a loss over the SE(3) manifold. GDR-Net [ 13 ] regresses poses using a differentiable patch-based PnP, while follow-up work, GDRNPP [ 3], achieves state-of-the-art performance by incorporating stronger backbones and domain randomization. These methods suffer from supervision ambiguity when han-dling symmetric objects that possess multiple valid poses but are typically assigned only a single ground-truth pose during training. By formulating Flose as conditional flow matching in R3, we mitigate sensitivity to local correspondence out-liers through dense displacement regression and resolve the rotational ambiguities common in SE(3) -based losses. 

Generative methods for 6D pose estimation use diffu-sion [ 28 ] or flow matching [ 21 ] to model a distribution over valid 6D poses rather than regressing a single predic-tion [ 29 , 30 , 31 ]. Their primary focus is modeling pose ambiguity caused by occlusions (e.g., a mug with an occluded handle) or shape symmetries (e.g., a cereal box, a tuna can) by generating multiple pose hypotheses via stochastic sampling. DiffusionNOCS [ 29 ] learns to denoise NOCS [ 32 ] coordinates to recover 6D poses. GenPose [ 30 ] frames pose estimation as a conditional diffusion process, while SE(3)-PoseFlow [ 31 ]applies flow matching on the SE(3) manifold. These methods operate in the category-level setting, relying on weaker super-vision signals than instance-level approaches. Consequently, a direct quantitative comparison with Flose is not applicable, as we target accurate pose estimation where explicit 3D models of object instances are available. 

Flow matching for 3D tasks. Flow matching [ 21 ] has proven effective for shape assembly [ 18 , 19 ] and registration [ 20 ]by modeling alignment as generative denoising. GARF [ 19 ]applies flow matching over the SE(3) manifold to generate relative poses, RPF [ 18 ] operates directly in R3 to learn point cloud denoising without rigidity constraints. RAP [ 20 ] builds upon RPF by incorporating rigidity constraints into the Euler integration scheme during inference. Similarly to RPF and RAP, we employ flow matching in R3. However, we address the distinct task of object 6D pose estimation. Unlike 3D registration, which typically aligns two point cloud fragments from the same sensor, 6D pose estimation requires aligning a clean, complete CAD model with a noisy, partial real-world observation subject to clutter and occlusion. 

3. METHOD Problem formulation. Given the 3D model of a query object 

and an RGBD image I of a scene containing it, our goal is to recover the 6D pose of the object with respect to the camera ref-erence frame. We define the target object as the partial observa-tion of the query object visible in I. Let Q ∈ RN ×3 denote the point cloud of the query object, and let T ∈ RN ×3 be the point cloud obtained by lifting the pixels corresponding to the target object into 3D space using the camera’s intrinsic parameters. For notational simplicity, we assume the point clouds have the same number of points N . We aim to estimate the six degrees-of-freedom rigid transformation ( ˆR ∈ SO(3) , ˆt ∈ R3) that aligns Q with T , such that Qr = ˆRQ + ˆ t ≈ T . For the alignment process, we also define T r as the target point cloud transformed into the query’s canonical frame via ground truth transformation. 

Overview. We design a three-stage pipeline as in Fig. 1. We ex-tract point-wise features FQ, FT from Q, T by fusing overlap-aware geometric features and appearance-aware semantic fea-tures extracted with different encoders ΦΘ and Γ. We deform 

T into ˆT ≈ T r using a flow matching model ΨΩ conditioned on these features. We estimate the rigid transformation (R, t)

that best aligns T with ˆT by solving a Procrustes problem. 

Feature encoding extracts point-level features F by combining overlap-aware features O and semantic features S via point-wise addition. The overlap-aware encoder ΦΘ is a paramet-ric neural network with learnable parameters Θ. It takes 3D points (and their normals, estimated via PCA over local neigh-borhood) as input and predicts which points belong to the overlapping region between Q and T r . Applying ΦΘ to Q

and T produce binary classification logits LQ, LT ∈ RN ×2.During training, the parameters Θ are optimized by minimiz-ing a binary cross-entropy loss between the predicted logits 

LQ, LT and the ground-truth binary labels yQ, yT ∈ [0 , 1] N ,which indicate which points of Q and T belong to the overlap region. yQ, yT are constructed via nearest-neighbor search in Euclidean space: during training, T is first transformed to the query frame using the ground-truth transformation to obtain T r , then pairwise distances between Q and T r are com-puted, and points with distances below a threshold ϵ = 1% 

of the object’s diameter are labeled as overlapping. During inference, the classification head of ΦΘ is discarded, and only the hidden features OQ, OT ∈ RN ×F are retained, yielding overlap-aware features. However, relying solely on the geo-metric cues is insufficient for resolving ambiguities in objects with symmetries and sparse geometric details. X(0). Pose estimation: the 6D pose ˆR,ˆt is recovered via RANSAC-based Kabsch solver followed by ICP refinement. which associates pixel-level features from a frozen Vision Foundation Model (VFM) to the 3D points of Q and T . For the target object, the VFM is applied to a regular crop of I

centered on its barycenter and uses the known pixel-to-point correspondence to associate the pixel-level features with the points of T , producing point-level features ST ∈ RN ×G,where G is the dimensionality of the VFM features. For the query object, the encoder generates multi-view render-ings {Ji} of its 3D model, applies the VFM to the synthetic images, establishes pixel-to-point correspondences using the synthetic camera intrinsics, and associates the pixel-level fea-tures to the corresponding 3D points. If multiple pixels map to the same 3D point, their features are fused via average pooling. This process is performed once per object during pre-processing; at inference, the query features SQ ∈ RN ×G

are loaded from memory. The dimensionality of SQ and ST is reduced to F using PCA, to match that of the overlap-aware features. Specifically, we compute the PCA basis only on 

SQ and apply this projection to both SQ and all instances of ST . Finally, the output features are defined by combin-ing overlap-aware and semantic features via point-wise addi-tion, i.e., F = norm(norm( O) + norm( S)) ∈ RN ×F , where 

norm( ·) denotes the operator that normalizes each point fea-ture to have unit L2 norm. 

Conditional flow matching. We use conditional flow match-ing framework [ 18 ] to transform source point cloud X(0) to tar-get X(1) via linear interpolation X(t) = (1 − t)X(0) + tX(1) ,

t ∈ [0 , 1] . In our setting, X(0) = Q ∪ T r ∈ R2N ×3, while 

X(1) ∈ R2N ×3 is obtained by uniformly sampling Gaus-sian noise in 3D space. The neural network ΨΩ(t, X(t)|C)

learns to reverse the RPF process by predicting a vector field 

V ≈ X(0) − X(t) ∈ R2N ×3 that transforms X(t) into X(0) .

ΨΩ is conditioned on overlap-aware and semantic features C.The parameters Ω are optimized using the conditional flow matching loss [ 18 ]. At inference time, we perform K uniform Euler steps as ˆX(t − ∆t) = ˆ X(t) − ΨΩ(t, X(t)|C)∆ t, where 

∆t = 1 /K . After K iterations, the resulting ˆX(0) = Q ∪ ˆT

approximates X(0) = Q ∪ T r . In practice, we replace the components of V corresponding to the anchor Q with a null field and apply the resulting velocity field to T to obtain a deformed version ˆT = T + V ≈ T r . Unlike [ 18 ], which conditions ΨΩ solely on geometric positional encoding, we enrich the conditioning signal C ∈ RN ×(F +H) with overlap-aware and semantic point-wise features that jointly capture geometric structure and object appearance. Specifically, C =[F ∈ RN ×F |P ∈ RN ×H ], where F are extracted by the feature encoder, P are obtained by the positional encoder Ξ, and | de-notes concatenation along the last dimension. For each point, 

Ξ encodes a 10-dimensional array composed of concatenated coordinates, normals, the coordinates of the corresponding point in the noisy initialization, and a binary scalar identifying either Q or T . In practice, Ξ applies sinusoidal embeddings with logarithmically spaced frequencies to each geometric attribute independently [ 33 ]. The resulting embeddings are concatenated with the learned point-wise features and a lin-ear projection is then applied to obtain the final positional encoding P used to condition the flow model.

We therefore employ RANSAC, where each iteration samples a minimal set of three correspondences to solve the orthogonal Procrustes problem via the Kabsch algorithm. Finally, we invert this transformation to obtain the standard 6D pose of the query object in the camera frame. 

4. EXPERIMENTS Implementation details. During pre-processing, the target ob-ject in the input image is segmented using the ZebraPose [ 26 ]masks publicly available on the BOP leaderboard. In the fea-ture extraction stage, ΦΘ is based on PointTransformerV3 [ 34 ]and produces overlap-aware features of size F = 64 , while Γ

is based on a frozen DINOv2-Giant [ 23 ], yielding appearance-aware features of dimension G = 1536 , which are reduced to 

F via PCA. Finally, these features are fused via point-wise summation. In the flow matching stage, overlap- and seman-tic features are concatenated with position-aware features of size H = 210 , derived from the positional encoding of object coordinates and normals, and noisy point positions. The re-sulting features of size F + H = 274 are fed to ΨΩ, which is based on Diffusion Transformers [ 35 ]. During training, Θ

and Ω are initialized with RPF [ 18 ] checkpoints and finetuned on the training split of each dataset in two separate phases for 200 and 100 epochs, respectively. Training is distributed across 12 NVIDIA A100 GPUs with an effective batch size of 384. During post-processing, we execute RANSAC-based registration for 1k iterations with an inlier threshold of 1cm, followed by ICP-based refinement for 3k iterations with a distance threshold of 1cm.

5. CONCLUSIONS 

We presented Flose, the first instance-level 6D pose estima-tion method formulated as conditional flow matching in R3.Flose conditions the generative denoising process on a fusion of overlap-aware and semantic features, and employs robust RANSAC-based registration. Experiments demonstrate that Flose outperforms state-of-the-art competitors, achieving supe-rior robustness to symmetries and occlusions while requiring less training and smaller inference costs. Our iterative for-mulation enables explicit control over the accuracy–efficiency trade-off by adjusting the number of Euler integration steps. 

Limitations and future work. (1) Our current pipeline relies on a two-stage training process, which could be simplified us-ing off-the-shelf geometric and semantic descriptors. (2) The iterative nature of flow matching hinders time-critical applica-tions; single-step denoising would enable these applications. (3) Flose depends on object-level segmentation performance; extending it to operate at scene-level is another interesting research direction. 6. REFERENCES
